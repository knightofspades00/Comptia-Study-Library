🤖 1.0 — How AI Actually Works 15%

📚 Learn more ▼

What is "AI" — and what is it not?

AI (Artificial Intelligence) is a broad term for software that performs tasks we usually associate with human intelligence — recognizing images, generating text, translating languages, recommending videos. Most "AI" you encounter today is one specific kind: machine learning.

  • AI — the umbrella term (1950s onward).
  • Machine Learning (ML) — a subset of AI where the system learns patterns from data instead of being explicitly programmed.
  • Deep Learning — a subset of ML using neural networks with many layers. Most modern AI (GPT, image generators, voice assistants) is deep learning.
  • Generative AI / GenAI — AI that creates new content: text (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini), images (DALL-E, Midjourney), audio (ElevenLabs), video (Sora).

How a Large Language Model (LLM) responds to you

When you type a question into ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini, here's what actually happens:

  1. Your text is broken into "tokens" — small chunks, roughly half a word each. "Hello, world!" might become 4 tokens.
  2. The model predicts the next token based on patterns learned from training on billions of pieces of text.
  3. That token becomes part of the input, and the model predicts the next one, and the next.
  4. This continues until the model decides to stop (or hits a length limit).

That's it. Every AI response — no matter how clever — is "predict the next word, over and over." There's no thinking, no understanding, no checking facts. Just statistically likely continuations of patterns.

📌 Key takeaway

An LLM has no idea what is "true." It generates the text most likely to follow your prompt based on training data. When it sounds confident and correct, that's because confident-sounding text was in its training data — not because it verified anything. This is why hallucinations happen.

Training data — where the model's "knowledge" comes from

An LLM is trained by feeding it enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, Wikipedia, Reddit threads, news articles. The model adjusts billions of internal numbers (called parameters or weights) to better predict text it has seen. After training, the parameters encode a kind of compressed pattern of all that text.

  • Training data has a cutoff date. ChatGPT trained before 2024 won't know what happened in 2025 — unless it has internet access.
  • Training data contains biases — over-represented opinions, gaps in coverage, mistakes. The model inherits all of it.
  • Training data is copyrighted material the AI labs scraped at scale — currently the subject of multiple lawsuits.

What AI can and can't do (today)

Pretty good atGenuinely bad at
Summarizing long textCiting real, specific sources
Translating between languagesMath, anything beyond simple arithmetic
Brainstorming variants & rephrasingRecent events (after training cutoff)

Other AI you use without realizing it

  • Recommendation systems — YouTube's "Up Next," TikTok's For You, Netflix recommendations. ML predicting what will hold your attention.
  • Image recognition — Apple Photos finding your face in 10,000 pictures, license plate readers, your phone's "Search your screenshots."
  • Voice assistants — Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant. Speech-to-text + intent classification + canned responses or web search.
  • Spam filters — Gmail/Outlook classify thousands of features per email to decide if it's spam.
  • Autocomplete — your phone keyboard, your email subject suggestions, Google search predictions.
  • Translation — Google Translate switched to deep learning in 2016 and quality jumped overnight.
💼 Real-world — why this matters for students

Understanding that AI is "next-token prediction trained on patterns" is the foundation for everything in this hub. It explains why AI is confidently wrong, why it's great at some tasks and terrible at others, and why the right way to use it is to verify rather than trust.

💬 2.0 — Prompt Literacy — Talk to AI Well 20%

📚 Anthropic Guide ▼

What makes a good prompt

A prompt is just what you type into an AI. But the quality of the response is mostly determined by the quality of the prompt. Vague question → vague answer. Specific, structured question → useful answer.

The five components of a strong prompt

  1. Role — who should the AI act as? "You are a high school chemistry tutor."
  2. Context — what background does it need? "I'm preparing for the SAT and struggling with stoichiometry."
  3. Task — what specifically should it do? "Explain mole ratios using a real-world example."
  4. Format — how should the answer look? "Use bullet points. Keep it under 200 words."
  5. Constraints — what to avoid or include? "No jargon I haven't learned. Show one worked example at the end."

Bad prompt vs good prompt

❌ Bad✅ Better
"explain photosynthesis""Explain photosynthesis for a 9th grader who's just learned about atoms. Use a real-world analogy and end with one question I should be able to answer."
"write an essay about WW2""Help me brainstorm 3 thesis statements for a 5-paragraph essay on the causes of WW2 from a European perspective. For each, list 2-3 supporting points I could research."
"fix my code""This Python function returns the wrong sum when the input list contains negative numbers. Find the bug and explain it in plain English before showing the fix. [code below]"

Useful prompt patterns

  • "Explain like I'm X" — "Explain like I'm 12 / a beginner / someone who knows basic algebra." Forces the model to recalibrate.
  • "Show your work" — for any reasoning question, ask the model to think step by step. Quality goes up significantly.
  • "Give me 3 options" — instead of one answer, ask for a few. Compare and pick.
  • "Critique this" — paste your own work and ask for specific feedback. Often more useful than asking it to write the work for you.
  • "What are the assumptions" — ask the model to surface what it's assuming. Reveals where you might disagree.
  • "What questions should I be asking" — when you're new to a topic, this opens doors faster than asking for an answer.

Recognizing hallucinations

A hallucination is when AI makes up facts that sound right but aren't true. They're the #1 risk of using AI for school. Warning signs:

  • Specific citations — "According to Smith (2019)..." The book or paper often doesn't exist.
  • Quoted statistics — "82.4% of teenagers..." Specific numbers feel authoritative but are frequently invented.
  • Confident answers to your edge case — niche topics, recent events, or anything specific to your school/teacher.
  • Code that "should work" — function names and library methods that don't exist.
  • Math beyond simple arithmetic — multi-step calculations are frequently wrong. Always verify.

How to verify AI output

  1. Ask follow-up questions. "Where did you get that statistic?" Often the model admits it can't cite a real source.
  2. Cross-check with a search engine. If the AI says "Einstein said X in 1923," Google the exact quote.
  3. Have it explain in different ways. If two explanations contradict each other, neither is reliable.
  4. Test edge cases. If it's a math or logic answer, plug in a different number and see if the method still works.
  5. Don't trust calculations. Use a calculator. Always.
📌 The verify rule

The right mental model is: AI is a fast, friendly, sometimes-wrong study partner — like a peer one grade ahead of you. You wouldn't copy that peer's homework without checking it. Same here. Use AI to explore, brainstorm, refine. Verify before you commit to an answer.

⚖️ 3.0 — AI Ethics, Bias & Academic Honesty 15%

📚 Learn more ▼

Where bias enters AI

An AI system doesn't have opinions — but it inherits them from its training data. Algorithmic bias is when an AI produces consistently unfair or skewed results because of bias in the data it learned from.

  • Representation bias — some groups are underrepresented in training data. Face recognition has historically been less accurate for darker skin tones because training photos skewed lighter.
  • Historical bias — past discrimination is baked into the data. An AI trained on past hiring decisions will recommend like the past did — even if the past was unfair.
  • Measurement bias — using a proxy that doesn't actually measure what we mean. Using ZIP code as a proxy for "creditworthy" can encode neighborhood discrimination.
  • Selection bias — training data scraped from the internet over-represents English, Western, and online-engaged voices.

Hallucinations as an ethics issue

When AI confidently states made-up facts and a student turns those in as research, harm is done — the student fails the assignment, the misinformation spreads to other readers, and trust in AI erodes. Hallucination is not just a technical bug; it's an ethical risk you carry when you use AI without verifying.

Deepfakes and synthetic media

AI can now generate photo-realistic images, voices, and short videos of real people doing things they never did. Deepfakes are AI-generated media designed to be mistaken for real.

  • Creating non-consensual sexual deepfakes is illegal in many states and a serious offense — including teen-on-teen.
  • Sharing them, even to "joke," can result in criminal charges and permanent record consequences.
  • Politically motivated deepfakes (fake speeches, fake endorsements) are an active concern in every election cycle.
  • Verification tip: reverse-image search a suspicious photo. Check the source. Look for tell-tale AI artifacts: distorted hands, garbled text in backgrounds, unnatural skin or lighting.

Copyright and AI-generated content

  • Training data: AI labs trained on copyrighted material (books, news, art) — currently being litigated. Outcome will shape what AI can legally read.
  • Outputs: the US Copyright Office has ruled that purely AI-generated work is NOT copyrightable. Human-edited AI work might be, depending on the level of human creative input.
  • Style mimicry: using AI to copy a specific artist's style without permission is increasingly seen as unethical, even when technically legal.

Using AI for school — the academic honesty question

This is the question your teachers are wrestling with. The answer differs by school, by teacher, by assignment. Some general principles:

Usually OKUsually NOT OK
Asking AI to explain a concept you don't understandPasting AI output and submitting it as your own work
Brainstorming ideas, then writing yourselfHaving AI write the entire essay
Checking your grammar / spellingHaving AI "rewrite" your draft into a different style
Asking for feedback on your draftSubmitting work where you can't answer questions about how you arrived at it
Using AI to generate practice questionsUsing AI on an in-class assessment without permission
Disclosing AI use when askedHiding AI use when policies require disclosure
📌 The honest student's test

Can you defend every paragraph of your work in a five-minute conversation with your teacher? If yes, your AI use was probably fine. If no, you've over-relied on it. Teachers can usually tell — partly because of detection tools, mostly because the student can't talk about their own work.

AI detection — what works, what doesn't

  • Detection tools (GPTZero, Turnitin) exist, but they're unreliable. False positives hit non-native English speakers especially hard.
  • The most reliable detector is conversation. A teacher asking you to explain your essay's argument exposes ghostwriting fast — human or AI.
  • "Make this less detectable" tools are also unreliable and academic-dishonesty-coded behavior. Don't.

Privacy and AI conversations

  • Free AI tools typically use your conversations to improve their model. Don't share secrets, passwords, full names, addresses, or anything you wouldn't put on a billboard.
  • Paid / business tiers (ChatGPT Team, Claude for Work, Gemini Workspace) generally do NOT train on your conversations — but read the policy.
  • School-issued accounts may have data agreements; ask your IT department before assuming.

🔍 4.0 — Evaluating Online Sources 15%

📚 Learn more ▼

The CRAAP test — the classic framework

For decades, librarians have taught a checklist for evaluating sources:

  • Currency — when was it published? Is the info still accurate?
  • Relevance — does it answer your actual question?
  • Authority — who wrote it? What are their credentials?
  • Accuracy — is the info correct? Are claims supported?
  • Purpose — why was it written? To inform, persuade, sell?

CRAAP is fine for textbooks and academic papers. It's less useful for evaluating random websites — because any site can be designed to look authoritative.

Lateral reading — what professional fact-checkers actually do

Stanford's research on professional fact-checkers found they don't stay on a page to evaluate it. They leave immediately and check what other sources say about it. This is called lateral reading.

  1. See a claim you want to verify? Open a new tab.
  2. Search for the source by name + "bias" or "controversy" or "wikipedia."
  3. What do other independent sources say about this source?
  4. Is the claim covered elsewhere by sources you already trust?
  5. Only THEN go back and evaluate the original.

The "trace it upstream" reflex

Most viral misinformation is recycled from somewhere else. When you see a striking claim:

  • Find the primary source. A news article quoting a study? Find the study itself.
  • Read the actual study, not the article's summary of it. Headlines and even article text often distort what the research said.
  • Check the date of the original. Many "outrage" posts cycle a 5-year-old quote out of context.

Source credibility — fast signals

Higher credibilityLower credibility
Named author with verifiable credentialsAnonymous or pseudonymous author
Editor / editorial process (newspapers, journals)Self-published blog with no editor
Cites primary sources you can checkCites itself or no sources
Acknowledges uncertainty / counterpointsAbsolute certainty, no caveats
Corrections policy visibleNo corrections, no errata
Domain you recognize (.edu, .gov, established news org)Domain you've never seen, lookalike domain
Date is recent for the topicDate is missing or very old for a moving topic

Misinformation, disinformation, malinformation

  • Misinformation — false content shared without intent to deceive. ("My uncle posted a wrong vaccine fact.")
  • Disinformation — false content shared deliberately to deceive. (State propaganda, organized political ops.)
  • Malinformation — true content used out of context to harm. (Leaked nudes, doxxing, real but cherry-picked quotes.)

The same post can be all three at once — depending on who shared it and why.

Specific red flags on social media

  • Screenshots of headlines with no link — easy to fake, no way to verify.
  • Emotional language designed to make you angry — anger drives shares; bad actors know this.
  • "Mainstream media won't tell you..." — often a tell that you're about to read something unverified.
  • "Just asking questions" / "Do your own research" — often a vector to push readers toward fringe sources.
  • Out-of-context video clips — short clips that don't show what was said before or after.
  • "Breaking" claims with no follow-up — real breaking news gets confirmed by multiple outlets within hours.

Useful tools for verification

  • Google Reverse Image Search / TinEye — find where an image originated. If a "war photo" was on the internet 5 years before the war it claims to be from, you've got a fake.
  • Snopes, FactCheck.org, Reuters Fact Check — established fact-checkers with public methodologies.
  • NewsGuard — browser extension rating site credibility.
  • Wayback Machine (archive.org) — see what a page used to say if it's been changed.
💼 Real-world — why this skill matters

Every adult in your life — including teachers, journalists, doctors — encounters disinformation. The ones who navigate it well aren't smarter; they just have the habit of pausing before sharing and verifying before believing. This habit can be built. The 30 seconds it takes is the most valuable digital skill you can develop.

🔐 5.0 — Digital Footprint, Privacy & Safety 20%

📚 Learn more ▼

Your digital footprint — what you leave behind

Your digital footprint is the trail of data you create just by existing online. Every post, every search, every location ping, every "agree to cookies." Some of it you choose to share (active footprint); some is collected without you noticing (passive footprint).

  • Active footprint — social posts, photos, comments, account profiles, messages you send.
  • Passive footprint — browsing history, search queries, location data, ad-tracking cookies, what apps you have installed, who else has tagged you in their photos.

What employers and colleges actually do

Roughly 70% of employers say they research candidates online. Admissions offices increasingly do the same. The thing they find that disqualifies you is rarely "you in a swimsuit at the beach" — it's discriminatory comments, threats, hate speech, or evidence of academic dishonesty in old posts. The cleanup:

  • Search your full name (first + last + city). What comes up?
  • Search for your usernames across platforms. Old accounts you forgot about can surface.
  • Audit old Twitter / Instagram / Reddit. Old jokes from middle school read differently at 22.
  • Set personal accounts to private OR clean them. Either is fine; both is better.

Personal Identifiable Information (PII) — what to guard

PII is information that can be used to identify, contact, or locate you. The more an attacker has, the easier it is to impersonate or stalk you.

  • High-value PII: Social Security number, date of birth, full address, mother's maiden name, account numbers, credentials.
  • Public-but-aggregating PII: Full name + school + sport + employer combined can identify you uniquely.
  • Don't share online: SSN, full DOB, home address, school schedule + location, your sister's/brother's name + school (kidnap-bait combinations).
  • Don't post: photos of your driver's license, passport, boarding passes, credit cards — even with parts blurred. People defeat blur and EXIF data often leaks the rest.

Passwords — the rules that have changed

  • Length beats complexity. A 16-character passphrase is stronger AND easier to remember than "P@ssw0rd1!"
  • One password per account. If you reuse a password, a breach at any site reveals it everywhere.
  • Use a password manager. Bitwarden (free, open source), 1Password, Apple Keychain, Google Password Manager. Pick one — any one — and use it.
  • Never share passwords over text/email. Even your friends. Even your siblings. There's no good reason.
  • Check haveibeenpwned.com with your email — see which breaches your account has been in. Change those passwords.

Two-factor authentication (2FA / MFA)

Even with a strong password, someone could trick or breach their way to it. Two-factor authentication requires a second proof — usually a code from your phone — so a stolen password alone isn't enough.

MethodSecurityNotes
SMS code⚠️ WeakestBetter than nothing. Vulnerable to SIM-swap attacks.
Authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy, etc.)✓ StrongCodes generated on your phone. Recommended for most accounts.
Hardware key (YubiKey, etc.)✓✓ StrongestPhishing-resistant. ~$25. Worth it for email + banking.
Biometrics (Face ID, fingerprint)✓ StrongConvenient and secure for unlocking devices.

Turn on 2FA for at minimum: primary email, primary social, banking, school account. The first three are the highest-value targets.

Phishing and social engineering

Phishing is an attacker tricking you into giving up credentials or money by pretending to be someone you trust. The volume is staggering — most data breaches start with a successful phish.

  • Red flags: urgency ("act now or your account will be suspended"), unexpected attachments, slight URL misspellings (amaz0n.com, schoolofficial-portal.com), generic greetings, sender address that doesn't match the displayed name.
  • The rule: never click a link or call a number from a suspicious email. Open your browser, navigate to the official site yourself.
  • "Smishing" — phishing via SMS. The IRS won't text you. The Postal Service won't text you about a missed package with a link.
  • "Vishing" — phishing via voice call. "This is Microsoft, your computer is infected." It is not.
  • AI-enhanced phishing: attackers now use AI to write fluent, personalized phishing. The old advice ("look for typos") no longer works.

Privacy settings you should know about

  • Location services — most apps don't need always-on location. Set to "While using app" or "Ask each time."
  • Microphone / camera permissions — review which apps have access. Revoke anything you don't actively need.
  • Ad tracking — iOS: Settings → Privacy → Tracking → "Allow Apps to Request to Track" off. Android: Settings → Privacy → Ads → Reset advertising ID + opt out.
  • Social media defaults — public profile? Public friend list? Public photo tags? Audit each platform.
  • Browser tracking — Firefox has solid tracking protection on by default. Brave is also strong. Use private/incognito for sensitive searches.

If something bad happens online

  1. Save evidence first — screenshots before blocking or reporting.
  2. Don't engage — replying gives the harasser oxygen.
  3. Use the platform's report function. Most respond faster than people assume.
  4. Tell a trusted adult. Always.
  5. Threats or sexual content involving minors — call Cybertipline 1-800-843-5678 (US) or your country's equivalent. Local police for immediate threats.
📌 The 10-minute privacy audit

Once per semester: (1) check haveibeenpwned for your email, change any passwords flagged. (2) confirm 2FA is on for email + main social. (3) review app permissions on your phone — revoke anything weird. (4) search your name on Google. (5) review who you're following / who's following you on each platform.

🌱 6.0 — Digital Citizenship & Wellbeing 15%

📚 Learn more ▼

What "digital citizenship" actually means

You're a citizen of physical communities (school, town, country) and digital ones (group chats, classes, online games, social platforms). Digital citizenship is acting like a thoughtful member of those communities — treating people decently, protecting yourself, protecting others, contributing rather than just consuming.

Communication etiquette across channels

  • Tone reads worse in text than you intended. No facial expressions, no inflection. Add a "haha" or an emoji or a longer message when something might land sharply.
  • ALL CAPS reads as shouting. Always.
  • Replying within a group chat is one-to-many. Anything you wouldn't announce in front of everyone, don't put in the chat.
  • Group chats are forever. Screenshots leave the chat. Assume anything sent could be public someday.
  • Don't @-mention people for sport. If you wouldn't walk up to them and say it, don't broadcast it.
  • Different platforms have different norms. Email is formal-ish. Slack is fast-and-casual. Twitter is brutal. Discord is its own subculture per server. Read before you post.

Cyberbullying — recognizing and responding

Cyberbullying is repeated harm via digital tools — texts, posts, calls, group chats, gaming. It looks different from in-person bullying (no fists, no playground) but the consequences are real, including measurable mental health harm.

  • If you're the target: don't engage. Save evidence. Block. Report to platform. Tell a trusted adult.
  • If you witness it: don't pile on, even with a "haha." A single person speaking up ("not cool") often stops it.
  • If you find yourself participating: step back. The crowd dynamic of group chats makes good people do mean things. Be the one who doesn't.
  • If you're the one being mean: own it, apologize, change. Most teen cyberbullying is impulsive — not evil. The fastest recovery is honesty.

Mental health and screen time

The research on phones and teen mental health is mixed but a few patterns are robust:

  • Sleep before screens. Phones in the bedroom predict shorter, worse sleep. A cheap alarm clock + leaving the phone in another room helps measurably.
  • Notifications fragment attention. Turn off all but the most important. iOS Focus Modes / Android Do Not Disturb are designed for this.
  • Social media + comparison. Curated highlights from people you know make your ordinary days feel worse. Following strangers is often less harmful than following people you compete with.
  • Doomscrolling. The infinite-scroll feed is designed to outlast your willpower. Use the screen-time limits on iOS / Android — even if you bypass them, the friction is real.

Ergonomics — your body matters

Tech neck, repetitive strain, dry eyes are all real. The fixes are stupidly simple:

  • Screen at eye level. Looking down at a laptop for hours wrecks your neck. Raise it (books, monitor stand) and use an external keyboard.
  • 20-20-20 rule. Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Resets eye strain.
  • Wrists straight. When typing, your wrists should be flat, not bent up or down.
  • Get up every hour. Walk 2 minutes. Drink water. Look out a window.
  • Hearing — turn it down. Headphones at 80%+ for hours is the recipe for tinnitus and hearing loss by age 30. iOS / Android can show your dose. Heed the warning.

Healthy AI habits

  • Use AI to learn, not to skip learning. "Explain this to me" is great. "Do this for me" undercuts the point of the assignment.
  • Disclose when policies require it. Honesty is the long-game right answer.
  • Don't outsource thinking. The skill of struggling with a problem and arriving at an answer is what builds your brain. AI shortcuts that skill.
  • Watch the dependency. If you can't draft, plan, or research without AI, you've traded a real skill for a brittle one.

Being a good online citizen

  • Credit creators. Repost with attribution. "Found on Twitter" isn't a source. Link to the original.
  • Don't pile on. Mass attention can ruin a person. If 10,000 people are already roasting someone, you don't need to be 10,001.
  • Hype people up when they make stuff. A real comment on a friend's first short film matters more than 100 likes.
  • Apologize when you're wrong online. It defuses 90% of fights. Most teens don't do it; it stands out.
  • Don't share what you wouldn't want shared about yourself. Including screenshots of private conversations, embarrassing photos, secrets.
💼 Real-world — the long game

The internet is forever. The kid who sent that nasty Snapchat in 9th grade is interviewing for a job at 22 and someone screenshots show up. The person who chose decency in their group chat is now a manager who trusts the people they hire. Both kids were 14 when they made the choice that shaped this. Be the second kid.

💬 Prompt Lab — 30 min

Activity 1: Bad prompt → good prompt

Pick something you actually want to know about. Then write FIVE versions of the same prompt, each adding one element from the prompt anatomy:

  1. Bare question: "Explain photosynthesis."
  2. Add role: "You are a high school biology tutor. Explain photosynthesis."
  3. Add context: "...I just learned about atoms. Explain photosynthesis."
  4. Add format: "...in bullet points under 200 words."
  5. Add constraint: "...with one real-world analogy and a check-your-understanding question at the end."

Send each to the same AI tool. Compare the responses. Which version actually helps you learn? Which felt most useful? Save the best prompt as a template.

🔍 Fact-Check Drill — 20 min

Activity 2: Lateral-read three viral claims

Open social media. Find three posts making striking claims — political, scientific, "did you know X." For each:

  1. Don't share or react yet.
  2. Open a new tab. Search the source's name + "Wikipedia" or + "bias."
  3. Search the specific claim. What do other independent outlets say?
  4. Trace upstream — find the primary source if it's a study or quote.
  5. Rate the claim: ✓ holds up, ⚠️ partially true / out of context, ❌ false.

Do this every week for a month. You'll find your reflex changes — you'll start pausing automatically before sharing.

🔐 Security Audit — 30 min

Activity 3: Lock down your accounts

One sitting. Bring a parent or friend if you want company. Walk through:

  1. Visit 🔗 haveibeenpwned.com — type your email. Note which breaches your account is in.
  2. For each breached account, change the password (use a password manager to generate).
  3. Enable 2FA on (at minimum): primary email, primary social, school account.
  4. Install a password manager if you haven't — Bitwarden is free, open source, works everywhere.
  5. Audit phone app permissions: Settings → Privacy → Location / Mic / Camera. Revoke anything weird.
  6. Google your full name + city. Note anything that surprises you. Make removal requests where relevant.
⚖️ Ethics Case Study — 15 min

Activity 4: Read & debate an AI ethics scenario

Pick one of these scenarios (or a real one from your life). Spend 5 minutes thinking about each side, then write a 2-paragraph response:

  1. A classmate used AI to write an entire essay, got an A, then complained when YOU also used AI (more lightly) and got caught. Was anyone wrong? What's the right response?
  2. An AI hiring tool rejects 90% of women applicants automatically. The company says "the algorithm is just learning from past hires." Who's responsible?
  3. A student uses an AI voice-cloning tool to recreate a deceased grandparent's voice for a family memorial video. Other family members feel deeply uncomfortable. Was this OK?
  4. You see a deepfake video of a politician saying something inflammatory. You verify it's fake. Do you share the fake with the disclaimer "this is fake," or not at all? Why?

Compare your answer with classmates. Notice where reasonable people disagree.

📱 Footprint Cleanup — 1 hour total, can spread out

Activity 5: Audit your social presence

  1. For each major social account (Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, Twitter/X, Reddit), open it and click through to your oldest posts.
  2. Read your posts from middle school through last year. Note anything you'd be uncomfortable with a future college / employer seeing.
  3. Delete, archive, or hide what you find. Update profile / bio if needed.
  4. Review your privacy settings — public vs friends vs private. Default to more private than you think you need.
  5. Audit your photo tags — who tags you, and what shows up? Untag yourself from anything you don't want associated with you.
  6. Document any accounts you no longer use — delete or archive them.

🧠 Walk Me Through It — Step-by-Step Skills

The three habits that change how you use the internet. Click any panel to expand.

🤖Is this AI answer trustworthy? — a 60-second check
Scenario: You asked ChatGPT for research on a topic for an essay. It gave you a confident, well-structured response with three "studies" cited. Should you use them?
Step 1 — What kind of question did you ask?

If you asked for: brainstorming, explanation, summary, rewording, generic code, opinion-style answer → low verification needed.

If you asked for: facts, citations, statistics, recent events, math, anything with a number you'll use → high verification needed.

Your essay needs citations. This is high-verification territory.

Step 2 — Test ONE citation

Pick the first citation: "Smith, J. (2019). Effects of social media on adolescent attention. Journal of Adolescent Research, 34(2), 123–145."

Open Google Scholar. Paste the exact title in quotes. Two outcomes:

  • Match found: the paper exists. Click through, verify the AI summarized it correctly (often it didn't).
  • No match: high chance the citation is hallucinated. Try the author + year separately. If neither matches → fake.
Step 3 — Pattern check

If the first citation is fake, the others probably are too. AI hallucinations cluster — when the model decides to invent, it tends to invent fluently. Don't trust the rest without checking each.

Step 4 — Cross-check the underlying claim

Even if the citations are fake, the CLAIM might still be true. Ask: "Independent of the AI's citation, is there real research on this?" Search Google Scholar or your library database for the actual topic. Use what you find. Cite that.

The verify rule

AI gives you a starting point, not an ending point. Treat its citations like a Wikipedia article's — useful for finding real sources, never the source itself. The 5 minutes you spend verifying is the difference between a confident paper and a teacher discovering you cited a non-existent study.

🔐Set up 2FA on your main email — 10 minutes that prevent 90% of takeovers
Scenario: Your primary email account is the keys to your digital life — password resets for every other service flow through it. If someone takes it over, they take over everything. Let's harden it.
Step 1 — Pick the right 2FA method
MethodSecurityConvenience
SMS code⚠️ Weak (SIM-swap risk)Easy
Authenticator app✓ StrongEasy with phone
Hardware key (YubiKey)✓✓ StrongestCarry the key
Passkeys (newer)✓✓ StrongestAuto with phone biometrics

For most students: authenticator app is the sweet spot. Authy is popular; Google Authenticator is built in to Android.

Step 2 — Turn it on (Google example — Outlook / iCloud are nearly identical)
  1. Sign in to your Google account on a computer.
  2. Top right → click your profile picture → "Manage your Google Account."
  3. Left side → "Security."
  4. "2-Step Verification" → Get started.
  5. It will ask you to verify your current password.
  6. Pick "Authenticator app" → "Set up."
  7. Install Authy or Google Authenticator on your phone if you haven't.
  8. The Google page shows a QR code; scan it with the authenticator app.
  9. The app generates a 6-digit code. Type it on the Google page to confirm.
  10. Done. From now on, every new sign-in requires both your password AND the current 6-digit code from the app.
Step 3 — CRITICAL: save backup codes

The setup process gives you "backup codes" — 8-10 one-time codes you can use if you lose your phone. Print them, photograph them on a backed-up device, or write them in a notebook. Losing your phone WITHOUT backup codes is how you get locked out of your own email permanently.

Step 4 — Repeat for other high-value accounts

Same process on:

  • Your main social account (Instagram, Snapchat, TikTok)
  • Your school account
  • Any banking / payment app (Venmo, Cash App, your bank)
  • Your password manager (this is non-negotiable)
  • Your phone carrier account — to prevent SIM swaps
Why this works

Most account takeovers happen because a password was leaked in some unrelated breach. Without 2FA, the leaked password = full access. With 2FA, the attacker also needs your phone OR your hardware key — usually a dealbreaker. This single hour of setup is the single highest-leverage security thing a teen can do.

🔍Lateral-read a viral claim in 60 seconds
Scenario: Your group chat is blowing up over a screenshot claiming "Scientists discover X causes Y!" with a chart. Before you react — let's verify.
Step 1 — Stop. Don't share yet.

This is the hardest step. The urge to react fast is exactly what bad actors design for. Even waiting 30 seconds reduces the spread of bad info dramatically. Just pause.

Step 2 — Identify the source

What's the website / publication / @-handle in the screenshot? If there isn't one — that's already suspicious. Real journalism is bylined.

Step 3 — Open a NEW tab. Search the source name + "Wikipedia."

Wikipedia gives you a quick read on:

  • What kind of publication it is (legit news org? satire? advocacy?)
  • Known biases or controversies
  • How long it's been around

If the source isn't on Wikipedia, that's also a signal — most legitimate news orgs are.

Step 4 — Search the actual CLAIM (not the source)

Type the claim into Google. Are other independent sources covering it?

  • Multiple credible sources covering it → probably real, dig in
  • Only the original + fringe reposts → red flag
  • Snopes, FactCheck.org, Reuters Fact Check has a writeup → read that
Step 5 — Trace upstream

If the claim cites a study, find the actual study. Read the abstract, not the headline. Most "study finds X" articles overstate or distort what the research actually found.

If the claim is a quote, search the EXACT QUOTE in quotation marks. Misattributed quotes are everywhere; the right citation almost always exists if the quote is real.

Step 6 — Decide before sharing
  • ✓ Holds up: share if you want, ideally with the primary source
  • ⚠️ Partially true / missing context: share with the caveat
  • ❌ False: don't share. Optionally reply to the original sharer with a link to the debunk.
The 60-second budget

This whole flow takes about a minute once you're practiced. The fact that 90%+ of people don't do it is why misinformation thrives — and why doing it makes you a noticeably more reliable friend / classmate / community member. People who routinely "fact-check before sharing" become the people others trust for accurate info. Worth the minute.

🧠 Tips, Habits & Careers

The habits that make these skills stick + where they can take you professionally.

🎯 Daily habits that build digital literacy

  • Pause before sharing. Just 30 seconds. If the post made you immediately want to react — that's usually engineered emotion, not signal.
  • Verify before you commit. Anything you'll cite, quote, share publicly, or build on — open a new tab first.
  • Use AI as a tutor, not a ghostwriter. "Explain this," "give me 3 angles," "critique my draft" → good. "Write this for me" → erodes the skill.
  • Audit yourself quarterly. haveibeenpwned, 2FA check, app permissions, Google your name. 15 minutes, four times a year.
  • Read tracebacks lateral-read style. See a striking claim → open a new tab. This is a habit, not a research skill.
  • Phone in another room when you sleep. Get a $10 alarm clock. This single change is well-documented to improve sleep AND mood for teens.
  • Curate your follows. The algorithm shapes you based on what you engage with. Engage deliberately with what you want more of; mute / unfollow what you don't.
  • Practice saying "I don't know." When you don't. Online or offline. The pressure to perform certainty is a major source of misinformation.

💼 Career paths these skills open up

AI / ML Engineer

Building the systems. Requires Python + math + a CS degree (or equivalent). PCAP is the foundation; understanding how LLMs work conceptually (this hub) helps you not be intimidated by job descriptions.

AI Ethics / Policy Researcher

Studying bias, fairness, impact. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, university labs, think tanks (Brookings, AI Now). Often values a humanities + CS combination.

Privacy Engineer

Designing systems that handle personal data responsibly. Demand is high — every company collecting data needs people who understand GDPR / CCPA / FERPA / HIPAA.

Cybersecurity Analyst (SOC)

Defending organizations from phishing, breaches, social engineering. CompTIA Security+ → real-world experience. Very strong job market.

Trust & Safety / Content Moderation

Building & enforcing platform rules. Discord, Twitch, TikTok, Meta all have huge teams. Real-time decision-making about misinformation, harassment, illegal content.

Fact-Checker / Investigative Journalist

Verifying claims for newsrooms, fact-checking orgs, social platforms. Skills overlap heavily with what this hub teaches. Growth area as AI-generated content scales.

Digital Literacy Educator

Teaching what you just learned, professionally. Common Sense Education, schools, libraries, after-school programs. Combines pedagogy with tech.

UX Researcher

Understanding how people actually use tech. Privacy and consent issues are central to modern UX. Studies suggest, "How does this feel to use? How does it shape behavior?"

🌍 Why this matters — the bigger picture

You're part of the first generation that will live with general-purpose AI as a normal part of school, work, relationships, and politics. The version of "literate" your grandparents needed (read text, do math, balance a checkbook) is no longer enough.

Digital literacy in 2025 means: knowing when to trust an AI, when to verify a claim, when to protect a piece of personal info, when to disengage from a feed designed to keep you scrolling, and when to log off and go outside.

None of these are advanced skills. They're just habits. The students who form them early have a meaningful, lifelong advantage over those who don't.
